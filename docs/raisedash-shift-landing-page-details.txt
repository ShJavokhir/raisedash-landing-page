Section 6
Compliance & Regulatory Context
This section establishes urgency by explaining why training isn't optional — it's mandated.
Section Headline
"Why Driver Training Isn't Optional"
or: "The Compliance Requirements You Can't Ignore"
FMCSA Training Requirements
Federal regulations require carriers to maintain documented safety training programs. During DOT audits and compliance reviews, inspectors look for evidence that:
        •        Drivers have been trained on company safety policies and procedures
        •        Training is documented with the five W's: what, when, where, who, and why
        •        Drivers understand Federal Motor Carrier Safety Regulations (FMCSR)
        •        Drivers are qualified to operate the specific vehicles assigned to them
        •        Training records are accessible and organized
Entry-Level Driver Training (ELDT)
Since February 2022, all first-time CDL applicants must complete ELDT from a registered training provider. This includes theory training and behind-the-wheel instruction. Drivers cannot take their CDL skills test until FMCSA has a certificate of completion on file.
CSA Scores & Business Impact
FMCSA's Compliance, Safety, Accountability (CSA) program tracks carrier safety performance across seven categories called BASICs:
        •        Unsafe Driving
        •        Hours-of-Service (HOS) Compliance
        •        Driver Fitness
        •        Controlled Substances/Alcohol
        •        Vehicle Maintenance
        •        Hazardous Materials Compliance
        •        Crash Indicator
High CSA scores (above 65%) trigger FMCSA investigations and can lead to:
        •        Increased insurance premiums
        •        Lost shipper/broker relationships
        •        Out-of-service orders
        •        Civil penalties (average $5,000+ per case)
        •        Operating authority revocation
State-Specific Requirements
Sexual harassment prevention training is mandatory in California, Connecticut, Delaware, Illinois, Maine, and New York for private employers. Many other states strongly encourage it, and documented training provides legal protection in harassment claims.
The Bottom Line
"Compliance isn't just about avoiding fines — it's about protecting your business, your drivers, and everyone on the road. Proper documentation is your best defense in audits and litigation."
